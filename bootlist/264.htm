3) Bandwidth And Traffic

This is a confusing subject to many people so perhaps I can try to explain it the best way I know how.

Bandwidth is, by classic definition, the maximum amount a wire/cable can transmit data in a certain time period.

For example, back in 1992, a T1 line was advertised as delivering 1.5 Mbps of bandwidth. At that time T1s were the "backbone" of the Internet. These days, 40+ Mbps pipes/cables are considered backbone. As one can see from the next article in this letter it's now common for home users to have 1.5 Mbps service at home. How times have changed.

Traffic, [aka transfer] on the other hand, is the amount of data that yourdomain.com is/can be, transmitted through a "pipe" for a certain time period. In other words, bandwidth is the size of a highway, say 10 lanes. Traffic is the amount of cars traveling on the highway.

Yes even on stats pages that keep track of the data your site serves will often say bandwidth instead of traffic [aka transfer]. Perhaps they really mean "bandwidth used" per total bandwidth available :)

When shopping for a webhost for your domain, make sure that you know in advance how much "traffic" you are "really" paying for. This is often noted in gigs/month. Note that many webhosters use the term "bandwidth" when they actually mean "traffic". And also note that some webhosts say you have XXX Terarabytes of access but they "may" really mean the size of the "pipe" they have, eg it may not be the amount of traffic you are allowed. Traffic is the amount of data that will be served to your visitors, including file downloads, for a certain time period, usually per month.

The new "Sales Hook". Some webhosters are now "offering" 1, or 1.5 TB Bandwidth/Transfer for $8/month. This is NOT possible. They are counting on that 99% of their new customers will never need such traffic allowance. Also kindly note that the wholesale price for 1 TB/month of traffic is about $100/month to the reseller so it's simply not possible to offer that amount of traffic allowance for $8/month. If you go with such a plan with false advertising and require loads of traffic chances are your site will go down without notice. Not good if your site is for business and you've paid for any service in advance.

Randy O. added:

Traffic will be the total amount of data BOTH IN AND OUT OF YOUR SITE. (Inbound is included in this number) This includes the email accounts you setup for user@yourdomain.com

The (sales hook). Some web hosts are now "offering" 1, or 1.5TB Bandwidth/Transfer for $8/month. This is NOT feasible for a number of reasons.

Physical capacity: Most shared servers are on 10Mps connections. This means in 30 days, the server could send out a maximum of 3200Gbyts of data (3.2 Terabytes) per month. So we, [ie webhosters, would be] limited to three $8 per month accounts [assuming the sales hook was true].

Worse yet, server slowdowns will occur at about 50% of this 3.2Tb per month, so as long as you are the ONLY account on the server, this is a great plan! Costs: Wholesale costs on 1 Terabyte data transfer averages around $100. So I get $100 worth of traffic for $8, another great deal! Reality: 99.99% of all websites use [less than] 10Gigabytes per month of traffic. If you try to use this 1.2Tb, you would be removed for using to much memory, CPU or hard drive time by % of use. (Read your AUP/TOS you will see if you exceed X% of resources, they can remove your account without notice)


*** A side note regarding email traffic. Several times now I've had clients who run small companies with 10 or less employees call me and say their website and email doesn't work anymore. Turned out the boss set very large allowances for the email accounts and the workers were using them to receive large videos and movies which took him over his monthly traffic allowance.

4) Bits And Bytes

Mfarcus telephoned - I just had DSL installed and they promised "Download at up to 1.5Mbps", but sometimes, it seems the service is only about twice as fast as my 56K modem. What's the deal? My DSL company says they are delivering the service I am paying for.


*** Let's go over the basics first. bps means "bits per second". Bps means "bytes per second". There are 8 bits per Byte. Chances are your "traffic" monitor is reporting in Bps but your ISP advertised in bps. So:

1.5Mbps DSL = 1,500,000 bits per second = 187,500 Bytes per second

56K Modem = 56,000 bits per second or 7,000 Bytes per second

My cable modem average = 925,000 bits per second = 115,600 Bytes per second

Often tho, I only get 600,000 bits per second at popular download sites. Other times even way less. Your download speed is dependent on the quality of the server you are downloading from and the number of jumps the file has to make along the way. For example, it's not uncommon for a download to have to go through 10 computers before it gets to your computer. Combine that with an "overloaded" source webpage server and one can easily see how your actual download speed could be way less than the promised "maximum download at up to" statement.

Randy O. added:

Transfer will only be as fast as the slowest connection in the chain.

Is a fantastic little utility that allows user of supported laptops to reduce level of noise on their laptops and to prolong battery life. On some laptops NHC may not work. Also please note that program is still in BETA, but if it works you are one lucky laptop owner. You also have to have Microsoft NET Framework 2.0 installed for this program to work.
